We maintain a continuously-updated catalog of AI tools at [RightAIChoice](/), scored
independently on features, pricing, and real user sentiment. We pulled the numbers on
**1,994 published AI tools** (June 2026) to see what the market actually looks like
underneath the launch hype. A few findings surprised us.

## Key findings

- **39% of AI tools won't tell you the price.** 772 tools (38.7%) publish no upfront
  pricing at all — "contact sales" only.
- **But 46% offer a free way in.** Freemium (38.5%) plus fully free (7.2%) means nearly
  half let you start without a credit card. Only 15.5% are paid with public pricing.
- **The market is API-first.** 54% of tools (1,071) ship a public API — AI tools are
  increasingly built to plug into other software, not just to be used standalone.
- **Half are built for beginners.** 49% are pitched at non-technical users; only 13%
  require advanced skills.
- **A handful of foundation models power everything.** Of tools that disclose a model,
  OpenAI's GPT family leads, with Anthropic's Claude and Google's Gemini close behind.

## 1. The pricing-transparency problem

The single most striking number: **772 AI tools — 38.7% of the catalog — don't list a
price.** They route you to "contact sales" or an enterprise quote. For a category that
markets itself on speed and self-service, nearly four in ten tools make you book a call
to find out what you'll pay.

- **Contact sales / no public price** — 772 tools (38.7%)
- **Freemium** — 768 (38.5%)
- **Paid, public price** — 310 (15.5%)
- **Free** — 144 (7.2%)

The flip side is genuinely good for buyers: **46% offer a free or freemium tier**, so
most categories have a no-cost way to evaluate before you commit. The takeaway for
buyers — and the reason we surface total monthly cost on every tool — is that headline
"free" often coexists with opaque enterprise pricing one tier up.

## 2. AI tools are now API-first

**54% (1,071) of tools expose a public API.** That's a quiet but important shift: AI
tools increasingly assume they're one node in a larger stack, not the whole workflow.
It's why "which tools integrate with what" has become as important as raw capability —
and why we plan stacks rather than recommend single tools.

## 3. Who they're built for

- **Beginner: 49.3%**
- **Intermediate: 37.7%**
- **Advanced: 13.0%**

Nearly half of all AI tools are explicitly aimed at non-technical users. The "AI for
everyone" wave is real in the data — the hard-mode, developer-only tools are now the
minority.

## 4. The model layer: a few foundations power the field

Among the tools that disclose an underlying model, the concentration is striking. The
most common foundations are **OpenAI's GPT family**, **Anthropic's Claude**, and
**Google's Gemini**, with **Meta's Llama** and **Mistral** as the leading open-weight
choices. A large share of tools list only "proprietary," so treat this as a floor — but
the signal is clear: most of the AI-tool economy is built on a small number of model
providers. When a foundation model ships a new version, hundreds of downstream tools
inherit it overnight.

## 5. The busiest categories

Where are all these tools? The most crowded categories (a tool can sit in more than one):

- **Productivity** — 519 tools
- **Automation & Agents** — 510
- **Data & Analytics** — 483
- **Business & Finance** — 348
- **Code & Development** — 335
- **Marketing & SEO** — 319

"Automation & Agents" sitting second — neck-and-neck with general productivity — is the
clearest sign of where 2026 is heading: from tools you operate to agents that operate
for you.

## Methodology

Figures are computed from the 1,994 published tools in the RightAIChoice catalog as of
4 June 2026. Tools without a meaningful AI capability are excluded. Pricing model,
API availability, skill level, and category are editor-verified per tool; the model-layer
figures cover only tools that disclose an underlying model (many list "proprietary" and
are excluded from that breakdown). Category counts allow a tool to appear in more than one
category, so they sum to more than the total.
